STEP 01

Sodium Oleate Solution Preparation

Prepare four serial dilutions (0.01 M → 1×10⁻⁵ M) in distilled water. Allow foam to dissipate before volumetric make-up.

Foam suppression is critical — read meniscus at the base of the foam layer, not the foam top. Serial dilution minimises accumulated weighing error.

STEP 02

Krüss Tensiometer — Wilhelmy Plate Method

A platinum Wilhelmy plate is lowered to contact the liquid surface; the wetting force is recorded by the microbalance. γ = F / (l · cos θ_plate).

The Wilhelmy equation: F = γ · P · cos θ_plate where P = plate perimeter (2L+2t). For platinum at complete wetting, θ_plate ≈ 0°, so cos θ = 1 and F = γ·P. Surface tension is computed automatically by the Krüss K12 firmware.

STEP 03

Powder Preparation & Packing

Crush each powder (mortar & pestle) to remove agglomerates. Pack ~1/3 vial volume using reproducible force onto filter paper base.

Consistent packing is paramount — C is sensitive to void fraction. The vial is inverted so the filter paper base contacts the liquid, mimicking a porous sintered glass bed.

STEP 04

Material Constant C — Hexane Reference

Since hexane (γ = 17.9 mN/m) completely wets both powders, θ = 0° and cos θ = 1, allowing direct solution for C. Immerse 60 s, record Δm.

m² = [C · ρ² · γ · cosθ / η] · t

With θ = 0°, C = m²·η / (ρ²·γ·t). This material constant encodes pore geometry and packing density — it must remain constant between hexane and surfactant runs.

STEP 05

Contact Angle Measurement — Surfactant Series

Repeat absorption experiment with each sodium oleate solution. Use experimentally measured γ and solve for θ using the previously determined C.

Four concentrations of sodium oleate → four surface tensions → four contact angles per powder. Combined with tensiometer γ data, the Zisman plot can be constructed.

STEP 06

Zisman Plot & Critical Surface Tension γc

Plot θ (y) vs. γ (x) for each powder. Extrapolate the linear fit to θ = 0° — this x-intercept defines the critical surface tension γc.

Based on the mean cosθ, Powder 2 shows marginally better wettability by the sodium oleate solutions (higher cosθ = lower contact angle). However, the lack of trend consistency and high scatter for Powder 2 undermines confidence — both powders are poorly wetted under these experimental conditions.

1. Why are all contact angles unexpectedly high (>70°)?

Both theoretical predictions and Washburn literature for native starch (γc ≈ 30–36 mN/m) and bare TiO₂ (γc ≈ 40–55 mN/m) suggest contact angles of 20–60° with surfactant solutions at γ = 25–50 mN/m. The obtained values of 71°–85° significantly exceed these. Primary explanation: surface hydrophobisation. Cosmetic-grade TiO₂ and ZnO are routinely coated with dimethicone, stearic acid, or alumina for dispersibility — these coatings reduce surface free energy dramatically (Evonik Aeroxide T 805, for example, has θ ≈ 95–130° with water). For the flour, lipid content and protein coat on starch granules increase hydrophobicity relative to pure starch.

Additionally, particle swelling in starch is a confounding factor — as water enters, granule swelling alters pore geometry, violating the constant-C assumption and inflating apparent θ values (Holmberg et al., Surfactants and Polymers in Aqueous Solution, 2003).

2. Why does the Zisman extrapolation fail (non-physical γc)?

Zisman analysis requires a monotonic decrease in cosθ as γL increases, producing a linear trend that can be extrapolated to cosθ = 1 within the physical γ range. For Powder 1, the cosθ values are compressed (0.09–0.24) with no clear gradient across the narrow surface tension window tested (25–49 mN/m). For Powder 2, the non-monotonic scatter (cosθ oscillates) likely reflects inconsistent packing — C varies between runs, invalidating the core assumption of the Washburn method.

A more reliable Zisman plot would require liquids spanning a wider γ range, e.g., including n-hexane (17.9), diiodomethane (50.8), formamide (57.0), water (72.8) mN/m — the standard Owens–Wendt series — rather than surfactant dilutions of water alone.

3. Material constant C — validity and errors

C is a geometric packing constant encoding pore radius distribution and tortuosity. Variation in C between powder packing replicates is the dominant source of error in student Washburn experiments. Even small differences in tamping force (±10%) can alter C by 20–30%, propagating directly to contact angle error via ∂θ/∂C ∝ 1/sinθ — which is large near θ = 90°. The non-reproducibility of Powder 2 (TiO₂/ZnO) is consistent with a denser, less compressible particle system where packing is harder to control manually.

4. Washburn theory — other influencing factors

Beyond packing, the Washburn model assumes: (i) rigid, non-swelling pores; (ii) Newtonian liquid; (iii) no adsorption of surfactant onto pore walls during the absorption window. Sodium oleate is a reactive surfactant that adsorbs onto TiO₂ and ZnO surfaces via carboxylate–metal coordination within seconds. This dynamic contact angle effect — adsorption changing θ in real-time — means the measured θ is an average over an evolving adsorption process, not a true equilibrium contact angle. Ideally, a non-reactive, completely wetting reference liquid series (e.g., n-alkanes) should be used.

5. Formulation relevance — cosmetic pigment wettability

In the context of a colour-changing foundation system (e.g., Tagra CameleonCaps™ in an O/W base), pigment wettability governs dispersion stability, colour development, and skin adhesion. A high θ for TiO₂ in aqueous media confirms why cosmetic TiO₂ requires surface treatment and appropriate dispersants (e.g., polyhydroxystearic acid in W/O, or Tween 80 in O/W). The finding that θ > 70° across the sodium oleate series suggests sodium oleate alone is insufficient as a wetting agent for these inorganic powders — a mixed surfactant system or polymer dispersant (e.g., Carbopol + polysorbate 60) would reduce θ more effectively for cosmetic emulsion applications.
